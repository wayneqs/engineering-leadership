Operating guide

Leadership Thesis

Managers Teams

The leadership frame is straightforward: improve throughput, quality, resilience, and ownership together, and make the trade-offs visible instead of leaving them implicit.

  • Purpose and outcomes
  • Governance without ceremony
  • Trade-off clarity

What this means

I treat engineering as a system of work, not just a collection of projects. The job is to improve throughput, quality, resilience, and ownership together, and to make the trade-offs visible. The aim is an organisation that can change direction, ship safely, and absorb operational reality without thrash.

What teams can expect

Clear direction, explicit quality and reliability expectations, and a bias toward removing structural friction instead of asking people to work harder inside a broken system. Teams should expect me to challenge hidden queues, unclear ownership, excessive parallel work, and avoidable toil.

What management can expect

Transparent progress, evidence-based prioritisation, visible risk management, and a lightweight governance rhythm focused on trade-offs, constraints, and course correction. Management should expect direct communication when reliability, capacity, or estate health require a change in plan.

What success looks like

Delivery becomes more predictable. Incident recurrence falls. The estate gets easier to change. Teams spend less time on avoidable rework, handoffs, and firefighting. Planning conversations become more honest because uncertainty, risk, and operational load are visible.

Purpose and stance

Purpose

This is not a manifesto or a process library. It is an operating reference: the principles, behaviours, controls, and cadences that shape how engineering works under my leadership.

Leadership stance

  • I usually arrive to some mix of scattered ownership, hidden operational load, too much WIP, fragile delivery, and legacy drag.
  • I do not optimise for cosmetic process compliance, 100 percent utilisation, false certainty, or local speed at the expense of system health.
  • My default leadership style is clear expectations, direct trade-offs, and a preference for measurement plus observation over assumption.
Leadership commitments
  • I will not treat roadmap pressure as a reason to ignore reliability or quality signals.
  • I will not ask teams to absorb chronic system problems through unsustainable effort.
  • I will use measurement to inform decisions, not to create performative dashboards with no consequence.
  • I will expect teams and managers to surface uncomfortable reality early, and I will try to reward that behaviour rather than punish it.
Decision rules

Engineering North Star

Teams Peers Management

The North Star is a small set of durable decision rules. It exists to make priorities legible when speed, reliability, security, quality, and ownership pull in different directions.

Principles become decisions, then decisions become evidence.

The North Star matters only if it changes what teams do, what leaders approve, and what the scorecard shows.

1. Principles

Outcomes Reliability Security Quality Evolutionary architecture Psychological safety Ownership

2. Decision questions

Is trust at risk? Is this safe to change? Who owns it? Does this preserve optionality? Can risk be raised early?

3. Evidence

Journey metrics Error budget CFR and MTTR Ownership coverage Time to right owner
If the principles do not change a prioritisation call, a release decision, or a stabilisation call, they are not yet operating rules.

Principles to keep

The seven principles matter because they are usable. They need to read as rules that can be referenced in real decisions, not as values that only sound good in a document.

How they guide decisions

Each principle should improve a real trade-off. It should tell us what to protect, what to question, and what evidence matters when priorities collide. If it never changes a release, backlog, or stabilisation call, it is not yet doing useful work.

Expectations for teams

Teams should be able to explain how these principles shaped backlog choices, release safety, operational decisions, and architecture decisions. They should be able to point to a recent example of each principle in practice.

Expectations for management

Management should see the principles reflected in trade-off calls, not just in language. The document should make those calls legible across quarters so it is clear why work is delayed, stopped, or accelerated.

North Star as explicit operating rules
  • Outcomes over output: Roadmaps and reviews should discuss movement in customer or business measures, not just completed activity.
  • Reliability is a feature: Reliability work is product work when trust is at risk.
  • Secure by design: Security controls should be embedded in delivery flow rather than bolted on at release time.
  • Quality is built in: A team is not done when coding is done; it is done when the change is safe to operate and support.
  • Evolutionary architecture: We prefer decisions that preserve optionality and reduce future change cost.
  • Psychological safety: People must be able to surface risk, uncertainty, and failure without fear of blame.
  • Clear ownership and accountability: Every critical area should have a team that can decide, act, and be found quickly.
Trade-off scenarios

Speed vs reliability

If reliability signals are red, new feature delivery slows until the service is back inside an acceptable operating envelope. This is not optional and should be visible in quarterly reviews.

Autonomy vs standardisation

Teams keep autonomy on product decisions and local implementation choices, but they adopt common safety rails, observability standards, and paved-road defaults unless they can justify a different path.

Urgency vs evidence

Strategic urgency does not remove the need for thin slices, staged rollout, and validation. It changes the speed of feedback loops, not the need for them.

Execution model

Lean System of Work

Teams Engineering managers

This is how work moves: small batches, visible queues, pull where possible, explicit constraints, and regular improvement loops. The goal is less waiting, less thrash, and more predictable delivery.

What good looks like

Small batches move with visible status, limited WIP, fast feedback, and fewer queues. Constraints are surfaced quickly and dealt with directly. Work ages slowly because teams are finishing more and starting less.

What I expect from teams

Use pull-based intake where possible, make interrupt work visible, swarm bottlenecks, and close the loop on retrospectives with concrete actions. Teams should challenge large batches, unclear ready states, and work that sits blocked without intervention.

What I will do as leader

I will look for structural waste, not just local productivity: review queues, dependency delays, environment friction, approval bottlenecks, unclear ownership, and repetitive failure demand. When the system creates the same pain repeatedly, I treat that as a leadership problem.

Signals

  • Batch size down
  • Queue time down
  • Aging WIP down
  • Rework and defects down
  • Predictability up
Day-to-day operating rules
  • Do not continue starting new work while older work is aging, blocked, or waiting for attention.
  • Make queue time visible, not just active work time, because the queue usually explains the delay.
  • Escalate dependency and approval bottlenecks as system constraints, not just local frustrations.
  • Prefer improving the path of work over adding another tracking meeting around it.
TIMWOODS catalogue and how to use it

Keep TIMWOODS as an explicit anti-list. It is a useful lens for identifying why work is slow or fragile without immediately blaming teams.

  • Transportation: Handoffs, ticket ping-pong, and context loss.
  • Inventory: Too much WIP, hidden queues, and half-finished initiatives.
  • Motion: Tool friction, manual steps, and constant switching.
  • Waiting: Reviews, approvals, dependencies, and environment delays.
  • Overproduction: Building more scope before demand is validated.
  • Overprocessing: Heavy ceremony, duplicate artefacts, and unnecessary governance.
  • Defects: Escaped bugs, regressions, and failure demand.
  • Skills: Silos, bottlenecks, and single points of failure.
Operating rituals
  • Visible weekly review of WIP, queue age, and blocked work.
  • Focused constraint discussion, not status theatre.
  • Retrospectives with tracked actions and named owners.
  • Regular go-see habits: observe real delivery paths, operational pain, and customer-impacting journeys.
Organisation

Team Design and Ownership

Teams Managers Peers

Team design is a control surface. It shapes cognitive load, coordination cost, ownership clarity, and delivery speed. The aim is durable teams, explicit ownership, and collaboration by choice rather than by default handoff.

Preferred team model

A small number of durable stream-aligned teams own outcomes end to end for clear domains or journeys. Enabling and micro-platform capability exists to remove friction and create self-service, not another queue. Team design should lower cognitive load and coordination cost by default.

Ownership standard

Every critical service should have a named owner, runbook status, on-call path, and clear escalation route. Ownership should include build, run, and improvement responsibility. If a team owns a service, it also owns the operational and architectural consequences of that service.

Interaction modes

Default to service relationships and self-service enablement. Use collaboration deliberately and time-box it. Use facilitating mode when a team needs coaching to build its own capability. Long-lived collaboration without an exit path usually means boundaries or ownership are unclear.

Manager accountability

Managers are accountable for team health, capability growth, operational load management, and risk visibility, not only delivery pacing. A team with unclear boundaries, constant interrupts, or poor ownership clarity is a management issue as much as a team issue.

Decision rights
  • Service-owning teams should be able to make routine delivery and operational decisions without excessive central approval.
  • Architecture review should be used for consequential change, shared patterns, and risk-heavy decisions, not as a gate on routine work.
  • Platform and enablement functions should define standards and support adoption, but not absorb all ownership for outcomes they do not control.
Ownership made explicit
  • The definition of team ownership: product outcomes, code, runtime health, operational readiness, documentation, and lifecycle care.
  • What counts as a healthy interface between teams: contracts, shared measures, service expectations, and clear turnaround standards.
  • What an engineering manager is accountable for beyond delivery: team health, capability growth, operational load management, and risk visibility.
Capacity and focus

Run vs Change

Teams Managers

Operational work has to be visible and funded, otherwise reliability and delivery compete in unhealthy ways. The model below is a default planning stance, with explicit triggers for when teams shift toward stabilisation.

Default capacity shape

The default mix protects roadmap progress, operational response, and deliberate system improvement at the same time.

Change 70%

Outcome work, thin slices, validated delivery, and product change.

Run / response 15%

Incidents, support, on-call, BAU, and urgent operational work.

Run / improve 15%

Debt reduction, toil removal, reliability uplift, and automation.

When error budget burn, repeat incidents, or interrupt load go red, capacity moves toward stability first. The model is a default stance, not a fixed quota.

Default model

Use a default allocation across change, response, and improvement so operational demand is visible and funded. Treat the split as a planning heuristic that adjusts for service risk, maturity, and current reliability signals. The point is to protect capacity for work that usually gets crowded out.

What teams should do

Track interrupts, protect focus, keep response work visible, and reserve deliberate capacity for toil reduction, maintenance, debt, and reliability improvement. Teams should know at any given time what is planned change, operational response, and deliberate improvement.

Stabilise-first triggers

When incident trend, recurring failure, error budget burn, or customer-impacting instability move into a red zone, teams shift capacity toward stability until the system is back under control. That decision should be visible and explained, not negotiated every time as though it were a surprise.

What management should understand

High utilisation is usually the enemy of predictability. If teams have no slack for response, improvement, or rework, delivery becomes more fragile and plans become less reliable, not more ambitious.

Default policy

The starting model is approximately 70 percent change, 15 percent response, and 15 percent improvement. Some teams will run a different mix because of service criticality, maturity, or inherited operational burden. The operating principle is to make that reality explicit and managed, not to pretend every team has identical capacity for roadmap work.

Operating policy details
  • Who decides when capacity is reallocated and how quickly that should show up in plans.
  • How on-call, support, BAU, compliance, and reliability maintenance are classified.
  • How teams report interrupt load and toil in a way that management can actually act on.
Failure modes
  • 100 percent utilisation presented as efficiency.
  • Invisible interrupt load distorting roadmap delivery.
  • Heroic firefighting normalised as commitment.
  • Improvement work repeatedly deferred until reliability debt becomes acute.
Strategy into delivery

Planning and Prioritisation

Management Product and engineering Teams

Planning connects company direction to team-level execution. It should preserve line of sight, make uncertainty explicit, and keep delivery decisions tied to outcomes rather than activity.

Planning horizons

Annual objectives set direction. Quarterly planning chooses the bets most likely to move the right measures. Teams then break those bets into thin slices, experiments, and staged rollouts without pretending uncertainty has disappeared on the way down.

Estimation philosophy

Estimate to support decisions about risk, sequencing, dependencies, and confidence. Prefer ranges and assumptions over false precision. Use estimates to improve planning quality, not to create an illusion of certainty.

What teams should expect

Evidence matters. Discovery and validation are part of delivery. Plans should be revisited when new learning changes the shape or value of the work. Teams should be able to explain why a slice is the right next slice.

What management should expect

A clearer line of sight from company goals to engineering work, plus explicit communication of uncertainty, trade-offs, and confidence. Management should see both the intended outcomes and the assumptions behind them.

Planning rules
  • Translate annual objectives into a small number of quarterly outcome bets with named measures.
  • Plan the next slice that can produce learning or value, not the entire theoretical scope upfront.
  • Revisit plans when evidence changes, not only when a fixed review date arrives.
  • Keep dependencies visible and actively managed rather than burying them inside delivery optimism.
Estimation in practice

Estimation is a decision-support tool. It helps us understand sequencing, confidence, cost of delay, dependency risk, and whether a slice is too large to manage safely. We use it to improve planning conversations, not to score teams.

Planning structure
  • Annual objectives and engineering translation.
  • Quarterly business review inputs, outputs, and decision points.
  • How bets become thin slices and experiments.
  • How rolling forecasts are maintained.
  • What not to do: big commitments without validation, roadmap certainty theatre, and using story points as a performance measure.
Minimum standards

Engineering Excellence

Teams Managers

This chapter defines the baseline for safe, supportable engineering. Quality, reliability, observability, release safety, and incident learning are not optional extras; they are part of how engineering work is judged complete.

Reliability and operability

Critical journeys should have defined SLOs, observability that maps to real user behaviour, meaningful alerts, and runbooks that support fast recovery. A service that cannot be understood or recovered under pressure is not production-ready no matter how complete the feature set looks.

Quality built in

The definition of done should cover testing, observability, maintainability, security, release readiness, and review standards. Quality is not a late phase and not a separate team. It is the set of controls and behaviours that keep throughput from turning into rework.

Safe change

Use standard CI and CD safety rails: tests, security checks, progressive delivery, rollback readiness, and traceable changes. Manual gates should be the exception, not the main control mechanism. If safe change depends mainly on heroics or memory, the system is not mature enough.

Incident learning

Run blameless reviews focused on system fixes, follow-through, and recurrence reduction. The goal is a stronger system, not a tidier narrative. Repeated incident themes should trigger operational and prioritisation changes, not just better write-ups.

Minimum standards
  • What counts as a critical journey and when it needs an SLO.
  • Minimum observability expectations for new and materially changed services.
  • Release safety expectations: rollout, rollback, and support readiness.
  • What incident review follow-through looks like and who tracks it.
Definition of done checklist
  • Relevant automated tests and quality checks pass.
  • Operational impact is understood and observable.
  • Security and privacy expectations are met.
  • Rollback or mitigation path is known.
  • Documentation and runbook impact are updated where needed.
  • Ownership is clear after release, not only before it.
Failure modes
  • Observability added after release rather than designed in.
  • Incident actions agreed but not tracked to completion.
  • Manual approvals compensating for weak delivery controls.
  • Quality discussions reduced to bug counts instead of system capability.
Enablement

Platform and Paved Road

Teams Platform or enablement functions Management

The paved road exists to remove friction from the system. It defines the safe defaults, reusable standards, and self-service capabilities that let teams move quickly without rebuilding the same controls repeatedly.

What the paved road is for

The paved road makes the safe and supported path the easiest path. It should reduce local setup burden, shorten onboarding, and raise the default quality bar. Teams should feel that adopting it saves effort rather than adds ceremony.

What platform and enablement should do

Build self-service capability, reusable templates, delivery guardrails, and coaching. Time-box enabling work and avoid becoming a permanent dependency layer. Platform and enablement should remove toil, not move it into another queue.

What to standardise

Authentication patterns, CI and CD foundations, observability defaults, environment setup, service templates, dependency hygiene, and operational controls. Standardise the things where inconsistency creates avoidable risk or repeated reinvention.

How adoption should work

Measure adoption, onboarding time, and friction removed. The point is whether teams can deliver more safely with less local overhead. If teams bypass the paved road, treat that as feedback about fit, friction, or trust.

Operating policy

The paved road should make common delivery tasks easier, faster, and safer. It should come with clear categories such as mandatory controls, recommended defaults, and optional accelerators. Teams should know where discretion exists and where it does not.

What good platform and enablement work looks like
  • Reusable templates that remove repetitive setup work.
  • Guardrails embedded in delivery flow rather than added as external checks.
  • Documentation that is close to the workflow it describes.
  • Measured improvement in onboarding time, provisioning, and deployment friction.
Questions teams should be able to answer
  • What is mandatory, recommended, and optional on the paved road.
  • How teams request improvements to the paved road.
  • When an exception is acceptable and who approves it.
  • How enablement work is measured beyond activity metrics.
Legacy and debt

Modernisation and Tech Debt

Management Architecture Teams

Modernisation is continuous estate improvement, not a separate rewrite programme. The aim is to reduce delivery friction, operational risk, and strategic drag without destabilising the business.

Default approach

Modernise continuously in slices. Use seams, contracts, strangler patterns, and hotspot retirement. Avoid framing modernisation as a separate transformation line divorced from product delivery. The best modernisation work makes product delivery easier and safer while it is happening.

Debt policy

Treat tech debt as a portfolio, not as an emotional category. Distinguish between debt that slows delivery, debt that increases risk, debt that raises operational cost, and debt that blocks strategic movement. Debt becomes manageable when its consequences are visible and comparable.

Budget and prioritisation

Improvement and debt repayment need explicit budget, clear trade-offs, and visible rationale. If they live only in aspiration, they will lose to short-term pressure every quarter. Debt work should be governed, not opportunistic or vague.

What management should understand

Visible modernisation progress rarely looks dramatic in one quarter. It usually looks like fewer hotspots, less delivery friction, safer runtime behaviour, supported versions, and gradual retirement of legacy choke points.

Debt classification
  • Flow debt: slows delivery or increases cycle time.
  • Reliability debt: raises incident risk or recovery cost.
  • Security and compliance debt: creates control gaps or support risk.
  • Strategic debt: blocks movement into a new product, market, or capability.
Avoid
  • Big-bang rewrites without progressive exit criteria.
  • Architecture programmes that cannot show a path to operational simplification.
  • Debt labels used to avoid prioritisation discipline.
  • Supported-version drift treated as background noise until it becomes urgent and expensive.
Working rules
  • Use a simple debt classification and scoring approach so trade-offs are visible.
  • Make replacement versus containment criteria explicit before larger investments begin.
  • Use ADRs to capture consequential technical decisions and the trade-offs behind them.
  • Show management visible progress through reduced hotspots, lower risk, and easier change, not just through larger programmes.
Guardrailed adoption

AI in Engineering

Teams Security Management

AI is part of the engineering toolchain, but it needs clear boundaries. The aim is practical adoption that improves throughput and learning without weakening security, reviewability, or quality.

Where AI helps

Use it to accelerate testing, refactoring, code review preparation, documentation, boilerplate reduction, and exploration. The goal is better throughput and lower toil, not novelty. AI should make the engineering system more capable, not less disciplined.

Controls

Secure inputs and outputs, human review for substantive change, traceability of generated work, and clear boundaries around sensitive data, credentials, and regulated content. Use controls that are easy to follow in practice.

Rollout model

Start with controlled pilots, defined success measures, and clear reporting on where the tools help, where they create rework, and where they should not be used. Build experience and judgement before widening access or hardening expectations.

Leadership stance

I want teams to build real experience with agentic and assistive tooling, but inside a framework that preserves security, quality, reviewability, and auditability. Curiosity is good; unmanaged adoption is not.

Approved and prohibited usage categories

Good starting use cases

  • Test generation, refactoring assistance, documentation drafting, exploratory analysis, and review preparation.
  • Constrained coding tasks where review and traceability are straightforward.

Higher-risk areas that need stricter control

  • Changes in security-sensitive code, infrastructure, data handling, regulated domains, or incident response.
  • Anything that requires exposure of sensitive internal material to a third-party system without approved handling.
Operating controls
  • Approved use cases and prohibited use cases.
  • Data handling rules for prompts, outputs, and stored artefacts.
  • Review expectations when AI contributes to production code or operational changes.
  • How pilot results are summarised for management and fed back into tooling choices.
Failure modes to avoid
  • Unreviewed code generation into production paths.
  • Copying sensitive material into external tools.
  • Adoption by mandate before capability is built.
  • Measuring success by usage volume alone.
Cadence and evidence

Governance and Scorecard

Management Engineering leadership

Governance should be lightweight, specific, and useful. It should make trends visible, support trade-offs, and help the organisation decide when to continue, correct, stabilise, or stop.

Governance rhythm

Each cadence exists to support a different decision horizon, not to generate another status meeting.

Weekly

Inspect flow, blocked work, incident themes, and immediate risk.

Monthly

Review scorecard movement, friction themes, and improvement focus.

Quarterly

Rebalance bets, stabilisation needs, and capability investment.

Outcome

Continue, correct, stabilise, stop, or increase support.

Inputs: scorecard trends, incident signals, delivery flow, operational pressure, and strategy movement. Outputs: explicit trade-offs and visible action.

Cadence

Use a lightweight quarterly review to examine trends, constraints, trade-offs, and risks. Back it with smaller monthly and weekly loops focused on flow, reliability, and delivery health. Every cadence should support a decision.

What the scorecard is for

The scorecard should steer decisions, not create surveillance theatre. Keep it small, trend-based, and tied to action. Use a mix of leading and lagging signals, and make sure each measure has a clear owner and an obvious reason to exist.

Measures to keep

Keep the scorecard intentionally small: outcomes and journey metrics, SLO and error budget, lead time and cycle tail, change failure rate and MTTR, security remediation, paved-road adoption, ownership coverage, and kaizen follow-through.

How management should use it

Ask what changed, why it changed, what action is being taken, and what support is needed. Do not turn the scorecard into a league table. A red signal should prompt action, resourcing, or reprioritisation.

Cadence and decision map

Weekly

Inspect operational pressure, blocked work, WIP age, and immediate delivery risk. Decide what needs escalation, swarming, or de-scoping now.

Monthly

Inspect scorecard movement, recurring incident themes, platform friction, and whether the current mix of work still makes sense.

Quarterly

Review outcomes, reliability, flow, quality, modernisation, and capability trends. Decide what to continue, stop, accelerate, or stabilise.

Scorecard definitions
  • Metric definitions, source systems, review owners, and expected trend direction.
  • What constitutes a red, amber, or green signal and what decision follows.
  • How scorecard conversations connect to prioritisation, staffing, and stabilisation choices.
Anti-gaming rules
  • Do not optimise for a single metric at the expense of overall system health.
  • Review measures in combination: lower cycle time with rising change failure rate is not success.
  • Prefer trend conversations over target theatre where teams learn to explain a number without improving the system beneath it.
Entry model

First 90 Days

Management New teams

The first 90 days are about establishing reality, tightening the system, and proving value early without overreaching. The goal is to create a credible baseline, reduce obvious friction, and build trust through evidence.

0-30: Baseline and align

Create a current-state view of flow, reliability, ownership, tooling, and debt. Agree the first version of "good" for service health, delivery standards, and scorecard signals.

31-60: Stabilise and enable

Make run work visible, improve incident handling, remove obvious friction, and introduce a few paved-road and safety-rail wins that reduce toil quickly.

61-90: Embed and prove

Run the first scorecard review, show movement on a few trend lines, publish the initial ways-of-working reference, and frame modernisation as thin-sliced and risk-controlled.

Leadership intent

Bound the scope, build a shared picture of reality, and prove that the leadership approach reduces risk and friction before trying to scale it broadly.

Core artefacts for the first 90 days
  • Current-state audit summary and top constraints.
  • Ownership map for critical services and journeys.
  • Initial scorecard baseline with known gaps.
  • Roadmap v1 prioritised by risk, friction, and opportunity.
  • Initial operating standards for SLOs, DoD, and incident follow-through.
What happens after month three

Steady-State Operating Model

Managers Teams Peers

After the first 90 days, the model has to hold in steady state. The aim here is a repeatable rhythm for running engineering without drifting back into hidden operational load, diffuse ownership, or reactive planning.

Weekly rhythm

Review flow, blocked work, incident themes, staffing pressure, and top risks. Keep it operational and decision-oriented rather than status-heavy. This is where short-term plan correction happens before problems compound.

Monthly rhythm

Check scorecard movement, review key bets, inspect platform friction, and decide which constraints or improvement themes need focused attention next. This is usually the best point for deciding whether a team needs help, relief, or a sharper change in emphasis.

Quarterly rhythm

Run the deeper QBR-style review: what moved, what did not, what got riskier, what needs reallocation, and which bets should stop, continue, or change. This is where the leadership approach proves whether it is learning or just reporting.

Leadership responsibility

Keep the system honest. Protect focus, challenge drift, keep trade-offs explicit, and make sure improvement work survives the pressure of day-to-day delivery. The leadership job in steady state is to keep the organisation from slowly reintroducing the waste and ambiguity that the first 90 days removed.

Steady-state chapter structure
  • Weekly forums and what decisions belong in them.
  • Monthly management and engineering review cadence.
  • Quarterly planning and scorecard review sequence.
  • How operational surprises feed back into plans and capacity.
  • How improvement initiatives are selected and closed.
What should feel different in a healthy steady state
  • Plans change for understood reasons rather than through surprise escalation.
  • Teams can explain their operating pressure in a way that management can act on.
  • Reliability, quality, and estate health stay in the conversation even when roadmap pressure rises.
  • Improvement work is visible, measured, and closed out rather than permanently parked.
